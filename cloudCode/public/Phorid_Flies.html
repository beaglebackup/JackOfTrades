Phorid Flies

Family Phoridae


“Hedges (1994) discussed phorid flies in detail and much of the following is from his text. Phorid flies are small flies up to 1/8 inch (3 mm) in length including the wings. They are usually tan to dark brown in color. A key character is the severe arch, or humpbacked shape, of its thorax compared with its small head, thus giving it the nickname:hump-backed fly”. The phorid fly lacks the red eyes of the fruit fly –the fly with which it is most often confused. The wings of the phorid fly also have two heavily sclerotized veins at the top of the wing, and the remaining veins are very weak and are not joined by cross veins. Adult flies have the peculiar habit of running rapidly along surfaces instead of immediately flying when disturbed.


Phorid flies are found throughout the world and are a serious pest in all types of food-handling facilities. They are primarily breed in and feed on moist, decaying organic matter and probably have the greatest variety of potential breeding sites of any type of fly. Examples include decaying bodies/animals, glue, paint, garbage, and drains. In fact, any material of an organic nature that remains wet can be utilized by this fly. Because they frequent unsanitary areas, phorid flies could potentially carry pathogenic bacteria and other microorganisms onto food products. These flies may also by referred to as “coffin” flies because they are common in mortuaries and mausoleums.


Trash containers that are not cleaned regularly are a good source for phorid flies. Trash containers that become breeding sources for flies are usually those that are not lined with bags. Phorid flies will also breed in any moist decaying organic matter trapped in cracks of kitchen equipment or under the base of such equipment where it meets the floor. Many restaurants, bakeries, and food processing facilities use hoses to wash the floor clean each night. Water under pressure can force food debris and moisture into the cracks under equipment. Phorid flies may deposit eggs at each point in the facility where this occurs.


Other sites where phorid flies may be found in structures include garbage disposals, rotting meat and vegetables, the over-watered soil of potted plants, and fresh-cut flowers in vases. They also have been found breeding in dirty mops in janitor closets and laundry rooms, animal feces, faulty septic systems, and human cadavers. Pet stores, zoological parks, and even homes where pets are kept in terrariums can experience problems with phorid flies. The flies breed in moist, decaying organic matter in the bottom of unclean cages.


The most difficult situations involving phorid flies occur when a drain line breaks or leaks under the slab floor of a hospital, hotel, or other large building. Over a period of months or years, wet organic matter accumulates under the slab and literally hundreds of thousands of flies can develop. Determining whether a sub-slab infestation is present usually requires cutting a core through the concrete to examine the soil beneath and to check for adult flies. Such an effort should be attempted only if a previous drain leak was present and repaired, a current leak is known, and/or all other potential breeding sources have been eliminated.


LIFE HISTORY. Phorid flies are a particular concern in hospitals and other health care facilities. Phorid fly larvae have been found in open wounds of patients and in laboratory petri dishes, resulting in contamination due to bacteria that might be found on their bodies. The tiny eggs are deposited on or near the surface of decaying organic matter. The female will deposited on or near the surface of decaying organic matter. The female will deposit about 20 eggs at a time; about 40 eggs during a 12-hour period. The larvae emerge within 24 hours and feed for eight to 16 days depending on the food source and temperature. The larvae then crawl to a drier spot to pupate under ideal conditions, the life cycle from egg to adult can be completed as quickly as 14 days, but may take as long as 37 days.”
