Pharoah Ant

Monomorium pharaonis

(Linnaeus)

Subfamily Myrmicinae


IDENTIFYING CHARACTERISTICS. “Workers are about 2 mm long, monomorphic, and have a two-segmented petiole. Their antennae are 12-segmented with a three-segmented club. They are yellowish or light brown to reddish, with the tip of the gaster darkened (Vail et al. 1994) They have a stinger, but are unable to sting (Edwards 1986). Queens are similar in color to worker but about twice the size. Due to their small size and color, Pharaoh ants are often confused with thief ants. The antennae of a thief ant, however, are 10-segmented with a two-segmented club.


BIOLOGY & HABITS. The Pharaoh ants are “tramp ants,” characterized by their transient nesting behavior. Indeed, their notoriety in pest control derives from their predisposition to “satellite.” An application of residual insecticides, for example, ofter triggers the splintering of a colony into numerous satellite colonies (Vail 1997). In cases where treatments are applied repeatedly. Pharaoh ant infestations can spread over an entire building.


Even without insecticides, a localized infestation of Pharaoh ants can spread quickly. Colony budding is a contributing factor, but the main cause is inadvertent transport by people (Edwards 1986). The ants can also forage long distances. In a military housing complex, for example, researchers fed them dyed bait and tracked their movement. Dyed ants were found as far as 45 meter away from the bait. They were found foraging in the vertical and horizontal dimensions inside the building, as well as outdoors (Vail and Williams 1994).


In temperate regions, Pharaoh ants nest indoors, but in the tropics they often nest outdoors. In their selection of nest sites, they are opportunistic, occupying any crack or crevice with sufficient warmth and high humidity. In houses, they are frequently found in kitchens and bathrooms near a source of water. Nests are often located in inaccessible spots such as interior wall voids, under floor, and behind baseboards or windowsills. In institutional facilities, there are found in kitchens, laundries, boiler rooms, and around toilets, sinks, heating ducts, and pipes. They are potentially dangerous in hospitals because of their ability to transmit disease organisms (Beatson 1972).


Pharaoh ants forage both day and night, often going undetected due to their small size and cryptic habits. Their trails, for instance, are often hidden behind baseboards, cabinets, and walls where the ants are using wires and pipes to travel, but trails sometimes become evident when they emerge from electrical outlets and plumbing fixture. Outside, trails are found around windows and doorways and following along the edges of bricks or siding. In one heavily infested house in Florida, the trails ran along the gutters and shingles on the roof, originating from more than 15 separate colonies nesting underneath the shingles (Hedges 1997).


Pharaoh ants are omnivores, feeding on fats, proteins, and carbohydrates (Edwards 1986). They scavenge and kill small insects, feeding on fats, proteins, and carbohydrates (Edwards 1986). They scavenge and kill small insects. To ensure a balanced diet, they have an “alternation” and “satiation” response to food (Edwards and Abraham 1990). Confronted with a choice between carbohydrates and proteins, colonies prefer he one they have not been feeding upon, and, when provided a preferred food for an extended period of time, colonies will then switch to other foods when offered a choice.”
