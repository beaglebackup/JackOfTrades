Fruit Fly

(Drosophila melanogaster)



BACKGROUND INFORMATION. Fruit flies are an extensively studied species and are often used for studies in genetics.


DISTRIBUTION & HABITAT. Fruit flies can be found anywhere fruit is allowed to rot or ferment. They are less associated with fruit decaying due to bacteria or fungi. They can especially be a problem in food storage and production areas.


IDENTIFICATION. Fruit flies are approximately 1/8th inch long with red eyes and an orange, tan, or dark body.


LIFE CYCLE. Female fruit flies lay 10-20 eggs in fruit, with an average of 400 eggs laid in their lifetime. These eggs, that are approximately 0.5 mm long, will hatch out 2-12 days later. The larval stage lasts 1-4 weeks, during which they will burrow into the fruit. They will then become pupa for approximately 1-2 weeks, during which time they will move to drier areas of the fruit or outside of it entirely. From egg to adult can take 3-4 weeks depending on environmental conditions. Females are able to mate 8-12 hours after emerging as adults. Adults can survive up to a year.


FEEDING BEHAVIOR. As their name suggests, fruit flies are associated with fruit and other decaying organic matter. Larvae eat the yeasts in fermenting fruit and are often found in association with rotting bananas, pineapples, tomatoes, pickles, potatoes or other decaying vegetables.


HEALTH CONCERNS. Fruit flies are often associated with unsanitary conditions and decaying matter, so there is always the potential for the spread of bacteria to food items.
