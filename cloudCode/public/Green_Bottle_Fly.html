Green Bottle Fly

(Lucilia sericata)

Background Information. Because of their association with decaying matter and well documented lifecycle, the presence and age of green bottle flies are often used to determine the time of death in forensics. The maggots are used to clean wounds of living humans in some medical procedures.

Distribution & Habitat. Green bottle flies are commonly found in temperate and tropical climates throughout the world. Green bottle flies are found around decaying animal matter, meat, garbage and animal feces. They are often an indicator of carrion nearby and are typically found around dumpsters. Green bottle flies are not generally house infesting pests, but if a large number are found an inspection should be done for dead animals, such as rodents in wall voids.

Identification. Adults range in size from 1/4- 1/2 inch in length. They metallic green, blue-green or golden in color. The back typically has dark hairs on it.

Life Cycle. Female green bottle flies lay 150-200 eggs at a time, usually in meat, fish, decaying animal matter or animal feces. Eggs are 1.5 mm in length, elongated and whitish in color. Females can lay up to 600 eggs in their lifetime, with up to 4 to 8 generations per year. If there is enough moisture eggs can hatch immediately. Larva feed on decaying tissue. Larval go through three instars and then crawl to drier areas to pupate. Pupae are in a harden case that starts white but darkens to brown or black within a few hours. They become adults in anywhere from 2 days to 3 weeks depending on external conditions. They can overwinter in any life stage.

Feeding Behavior. The green bottle fly is a scavenger. They will feed on flowering plants that mimic the smell of carrion and help pollinate those plants.

Health Concerns. Because of their association with decaying matter and garbage, green bottle flies are known to carry various pathogens. They transfer these pathogens by landing on food products.
