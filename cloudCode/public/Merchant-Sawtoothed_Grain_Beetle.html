Sawtoothed Grain Beetle

Oryzaephilus surinamensis (Linnaeus), Family Cucujidae


Merchant Grain Beetle

Oryzaephilus Mercator (Fauvel), Family Cucujidae


“The sawtoothed grain beetle is cosmopolitan in distribution. This beetle attacks cereals, dried fruits, breakfast foods, macaroni, sugar, drugs, dried meats, chocolate, tobacco, snuff, bran, rolled oats, brown rice, and walnuts, flour, meal, and other processed foods. It will infest figs and other dried fruits after prolonged storage. It readily invades packaged foodstuffs. The beetles and larvae have been known to enter every single package in a heavily infested grocery store. The insects soon became so abundant, it was necessary to suspend business.


Hedges and Lacey (1996) reported on a case in a home where this insect was found by the tens of thousands in a garage, originating from a large bag of birdseed. In home infestations involving sawtoothed grain beetles, birdseed seems to be one of the most common sources.


DESCRIPTION. The sawtoothed grain beetle is a small, active brown beetle about 1/10 inch (2.5 mm) in length. Its flattened body is well adapted for crawling into crevices. The margins of the thorax are saw-like, bearing six projections on each side, and are the reason for its common name. The male can be distinguished from the female by the fact that it bears a tooth on the femur of the hind leg. The wings are well developed, but no record exists of the beetle being observed to fly. A closely related species, the merchant grain beetle, Oryzaephilus Mercator (Fauvel), resembles the sawtoothed grain beetle and is known to fly. The merchant grain beetle has larger eyes than the sawtoothed grain beetle, which makes the canthus area of the head (directly behind the eyes) small and pointed.


LIFE HISTORY. The eggs are laid singly or in small batches and are deposited into some crevice in the food supply. Eggs may also be deposited in finely found foods, such as flour or meal.


The larva is yellowish-white and has a brown head. When fully grown, it is a little less than 1/8 inch (3 mm) in length. The larva thrives on foodstuffs of vegetable origin, such as rice and wheat, breakfast foods, chocolate, and nutmeats. Merchant grain beetles occur more often in seeds or grains high in fats and oils, like peanuts, and birdseeds. The sawtoothed grain beetle moves about nibbling hither yon, and probably cannot feed on the whole grain. It is therefore more often associated with other insects that feed on intact grain or in food products made from processed grains.


The larva molts from two or four times, depending upon temperature. Six to seven generations may occur annually. In general, development is more rapid at higher humidities; however, the egg and pupal stages appear to be affected little by atmospheric moisture conditions.


The insect will cease breeding during the winter unless the building is heated and moisture conditions are satisfactory. They may then breed at a slow rate throughout the winter. Since most heated buildings are fairly dry, little or no breeding takes place by the adults remain active.”


Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 770-771.
