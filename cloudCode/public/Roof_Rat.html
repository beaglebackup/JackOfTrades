Roof Rat

(Rattus rattus)


BACKGROUND INFORMATION. Also known as black rats, Alexandria rats, fruit rats and ship rats, roof rats are considered the most common rat.


DISTRIBUTION & HABITS. The roof rat originated in India Southeast Asia and from there were spread to Europe through the extension of the Roman Empire. They prefer tropical and semitropical climates especially those on the Pacific coast, Hawaii, and the Southeastern United States.

Roof rats nest in vegetation like trees or ivy and in structures, typically the upper portions of buildings. In buildings they are typically found in wall voids and false ceilings. In the absence of vegetation, they will burrow in the ground. Nests are typically round and filled with shredded materials, vegetation or cloth. Roof rats keep a territory of about 100 to 300 feet. IDENTIFICATION. Unlike Norway rats, they have a slender body that is 6 /12 to 8 inches long. Their tale is longer than their bodies, typically 7 1/2 to 10 inch long tail, as opposed to Norway rats that have tails shorter than their body length. They weigh about 6 to 12 ounces. Roof rats range in color from black to brownish gray and have a lighter underside. Roof rats are very adept climbers.


LIFE CYCLE. Female roof rats come into heat every 4-5 days. Once impregnated, the gestation period lasts 21-23 days. They give birth to 5-8 pups per litter. Females can mate again 24-48 hours after giving birth, leading to several generations to be born per year. FEEDING BEHAVIOR. Roof rats are nocturnal, searching for food at night. They are omnivores, and will scavenge from humans as well as eat fruit, vegetation, grains, seeds, snails, pet food and trash. They consume 1/2 to 1 ounce of food per day and an 1 to 2 ounces of water daily. Rats are neophobic and avoid new food items within their environment.


HEALTH CONCERNS. Roof rats leave 25,000 droppings per year, which leads for a high likelihood for contamination. Pathogens they carry include the typhus, Weil's disease, toxoplasmosis and trichinosis. They also can host other parasites like fleas or mites that may cause human harm or spread other diseases like the bubonic plague.
