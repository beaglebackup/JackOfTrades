Lice


Human Head Lice

“Pediculus humanus capitis (=Pediculus capitis), the human head louse, is a small 1/8 inch (1.8-3.3 mm) whitish species with faint dark markings on the sides. Female head lice glue small tan eggs (nits) to the base of hairs, mostly behind the ears and at the nape of the neck. An inseminated female lays one to 10 eggs every 24 hours, usually during darkness or periods of host inactivity. During her lifetime, a female will deposit about 80 to 140 eggs. The female first applies a bead of gluey substance to the hair and then a few seconds later deposits a single egg. Temperature and relative humidity are critical during the incubation period. At 84* to 93*F, 70%-90% of the eggs hatch in seven to nine days. The egg stage is somewhat more tolerant of cold than other stages, but egg mortality is high below 65*F and above 97*F. Even brief exposure at high temperatures is fatal (Busvine 1977). Almost immediately after hatching, a nymph takes its first blood meal and three instar stages are passed in eight to 12 days. Adult longevity may be up to 23 days (Nuttall 1917), but average life span is much shorter due to host grooming. The head louse is highly dependent on maintaining contact with its host, feeding several times per day, and cannot survive without a blood meal for more than one or two days at room temperature (Parish et al. 1983). This insect has been around throughout human history and is cosmopolitan in distribution.


Head lice are mainly acquired by direct head-to-head contact with an infested person’s hair, but may be transferred with shared combs, hats and other hair accessories (Meinking 1999). They may also remain on bedding or upholstered furniture for a brief period. In North America and Europe, children are more frequently infested than are adults, and Caucasians more frequently than other ethnic groups. An individual not previously exposed to lice will initially not experience sensation upon louse feeding, but once sensitized, may react with sever itching and eczema-like sin eruptions. Itching may interfere with sleep.


Lice are photophobic and mobile, making them difficult to located among the hairs. They are relatively uninfluenced by climate because their niche is regulated by the host, with constant temperature and moderately high humidity. Although they can survive short exposures to cold and heat, they will not grow or develop at cool temperatures but require conditions like their on-hose habitat. They soon starve to death in the absence of a host.


The bottom of the louse egg is glued to the hair shaft by an extremely persistent cement that retains the empty egg shell along after the young louse has emerged. Thus the hair of a previously infested person may contain numerous nits; though some may be viable, most are empty and harmless. Only presence of live lice can confirm diagnosis of active infestation. The presence of eggs alone is not sufficient for diagnosis as eggs may retain a viable appearance for weeks after death.”


Human Body Lice

“Human body lice (Pediculus humanus humanus) (=Pediculus humanus corporis, =Peiculus corporis, =Pediculus vestimenti) are closely related to head lice, but are less frequently encountered in the United Staes. The body louse is grayish in color and larger (1/7-inch; 3.0-4.2 mm) than the head louse. These lice infest humans, hiding in clothing seams and folds, contacting the host’s body only when feeding. Body louse eggs are glued to clothing fibers in the same way head louse eggs are glued to hair. Lice tend to aggregate along seams inside the garment next to the skin, producing large concentrations of eggs. The female louse depositions four to five eggs per day, for a lifetime total of 50-150 eggs. At a termperature of 75*F the eggs hatch in seven to 11 days (Leeson 1941); at lower and higher temperatures {99*F or greater] eggs do not develop or are killed. The nymphs mature in 11 days and the complete life cycle takes about three weeks. All stages will die within 30 days of leaving the host. They move off as the body cools after death, and may gravitate away from the body if the temperature rises in fever. Body lice are acquired mainly through direct contact with an infested person or their clothing and bedding, and are most commonly found on individuals who infrequently change or wash their clothes. A change into clean cloths, and laundering of infested garments (especially drying with high heat or ironing), are generally effective in eliminating an infestation.


As with all bloodsucking arthropods, the host reaction is more a feature of the host’s physiology than of any variation of the parasites. Pruritus due to louse saliva ranges from slight irritation to severe urticarial. Prolonged infestation results in a thickening of the skin with pigmentation changes known as ‘vagabond disease.’ Irritation caused by louse bites may interfere with sleep and scratching may lead to infection of the abrasions.


Body lice serve as vectors of certain human pathogens, including rickettsiae of epidemic typhus (Rickettsia prowazekii) and trench fever (Bartonella Quintana) and the spirocahetes of relapsing fever (Borrelia recurrentis). Interestingly, none of these disease organisms are transmitted by the louse feeding, but are instead introduced into the bloodstream by the host scratching contaminated materials into the skin.


Pubic or Crab Lice

The pubic Louse (Pthirus pubis) (=Pthirius pubis=Phithirus pubis) has a short crab-like body, whitish with a dusky patch on each shoulder, well-developed claws, and pale reddish legs. The broadly oval body is about 1/16-inch (1.5 to 2.0 mm) long. Pubic lice are most frequently found around the pubic region of the infested person, but may also be found elsewhere on the body (armpits or other coarse hair such as beards or eyebrows). Infestation by pubic lice is termed pthiriasis. The egg is smaller than that of Pediculus, and is glued to body hair. Female pubic lice produce approximately three eggs a day, and generation time is about 20 to 25 days. All stages are more sedentary than Pedsiculus, tending to settle in one spot, grasping hairs with the legs of both sides of the body, inserting mouthparts, and taking blood intermittently for many hours at a time. The legs are adapted to grasping large hairs and best positioned for rather widely spaced hairs, explaining its predilection for the pubic region. Because pubic lice are acquired mainly through sexual contact, their presence may be associated with other sexually transmitted disease. Pubic lice on a child may cause the physician to consider the possibility that the more innocent means, such as by sharing a bed with an infested person. Bites can cause severe itch but pubic lice do not transmit disease agents. Crab lice die within 24 hours after being removed from the host.


None of these types of lice can be found on any animal other than humans. Dogs and cats are not reservoirs for human lice.”
