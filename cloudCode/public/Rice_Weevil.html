Rice Weevil

Sitophilus oryzae (Linnaeus), Family Curculionidae


“This insect is rated by many entomologists as our most important grain pest, and it is worldwide in distribution. The rice weevil may have come originally from India and worldwide in distribution. The rice weevil may have come originally from India and spread from there on commercial byways and highways. The rice weevil is primarily a pest in warm countries and is important in the Gulf States and in the southern United States.


A few of the seed and food products these insects have been reported infesting include rye, barley, buckwheat, table beans, stored cotton, grapes, cashew nuts, cereals , wheat products of all kinds (e.g. pasta), and birdseeds, including corn used in bird control baits. This weevil can also injure apples and pears by sucking the juice and gradually forming cavities in the fruit, within which it then conceals itself. The larva must pass its entire larval period within a single seed or caked flour that must be large enough to permit it to feed until maturity.


DESCRIPTION. The adults of the rice weevil measure 1/16 to 1/8 inch (2 to 3mm) and are a dull reddish-brown having the prothorax pitted with deep, round punctures and with four light spots on the wing covers. These light spots are lacking in the closely related granary weevil. Another weevil almost identical to the rice weevil is the maize weevil, Sitophilus zeamais (Mot). It is, on average, larger in size and darker in color than S. oryzae. The round pits on the thorax are evenly distributed on the maize weevil while on the rice weevil, a narrow dorsal line void of the round pits is present.


LIFE HISTORY. The female eats out a cavity large enough for the egg, then turns, locates the cavity with the tip of the abdomen, and insets the fleshy ovipositor. The deposition of an egg requires only about three minutes, and the cavity is then sealed over as the ovipositor is withdrawn. The female rests a short period before starting another cavity. The egg requires three days for hatching at a temperature of 60*F to 65*F (15.6*C to 18.4*C). Generally, about 50%of the eggs do not hatch. The female may deposit eggs at any period of the year until within a few days of death, but egg laying during the winter is sporadic and temperature dependent.


The larva is creamy white with a brownish-black head. The larva completes three molts. Upon transforming to an adult, the insect will remain in the kernel from three to four days as it hardens and matures. Many of the weevils perish when trying to escape from the exit hold because they become wedged with only the head, legs, and prothorax free. In corn, the weevils will at times become so thick they can be screened out by the literal tons. In the South, many of the weevils overwinter in the field, largely within bird damaged ears of corn.


Both the rice and the granary weevil feign death by drawing their legs up close to the body and remaining still. This habit of feigning death is much shorter in the rice weevil because the rice weevil has functional wings with which it can escape by flying. The granary weevil must depend upon its ability of feigning death.”



Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 770-771.
