Pocket Gopher

(Thomomys bottae)

Background Information. Pocket gophers are also known as ground squirrels. They use their large front claws and incisors to dig into the ground. They are able to close their mouth around their teeth so they are able to dig without getting dirt into their mouths.

Distribution & Habits. Gophers burrow in the ground and prefer loose, deep soil with vegetation. They are commonly found in fields, parks and lawns. Burrows are comprised of a main tunnel with various lateral tunnels off-shooting from the main one. Burrows can go as far down in the soil as 5 to 6 feet and contain 200 yards of tunnels. Mounds of soil are the most common way to identify burrows from the soil surface. These burrows are typically only inhabited by one animal, except during mating season when the female is with her young.

Identification. Gophers are range from 5 to 14 inches in length, with males being larger than females. The range in color from nearly black, to brown, to almost white. They weigh an average of 3-20 ounces.

Life Cycle. Females become sexually receptive in the spring after they are born and may produce 1-2 litters per year. Females can have 1-10 offspring per litter, but typically average 3-4. The young are weaned at about 4-5 weeks. Gophers live on average from 1 to 3 years.

Feeding Behavior. Gophers are herbivores. They use their large incisors to chew grasses, roots, trees or shrubs. Most of their food items are found while digging their burrows, although they will travel short distances from their burrows. They are called pocket gophers because of the pouches on either side of their mouth they use to hold and carry food items. They are diurnal, active during the day.

Health Concerns. The most common problem related to gophers is the destruction they cause to landscaping, vegetation and underground utilities.
