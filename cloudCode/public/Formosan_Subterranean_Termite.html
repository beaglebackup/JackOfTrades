Formosan Subterranean Termite

Coptotermes formosanus Shiraki


“This subterranean termite is the most aggressive and potentially destructive termite species in the United States. In areas where it has become established, C. formosanus has gained notoriety as a devastating pest of structures, often displacing other native subterranean species. As a result, the Formosan termite has attracted a great deal of media attention and is worthy of more in-depth discussion.


The Formosan subterranean termite is so named because it was first described in Taiwan (otherwise known as Formosa) in the early 1900s. The termite is believed to originate from southern China, and apparently was transported to Japan before the 1600s. It arrived in Hawaii more than a century ago, and is thought to have reached the U.S. mainland in the mid-1940s on military ships returning from the Pacific during World War II. In 1965, a pest control operator in Houston, Texas, found C. formosanus in a shipyard warehouse in Houston. The following year it was also discovered in Galveston, Texas, and in New Orleans and Lake Charles, La. (Beal 1967). Infestations have since been discovered in 11 states (Alabama, California, Florida, Georgia, Hawaii, Louisiana, Mississippi, North and South Carolia, Tennessee, and Texas). Initial infestations of C. formosanus were found in coastal areas, presumably from infested ships or cargo. The termite has since been transported inland and limited infestations have been discovered as far north as Memphis, Tenn., Atlanta, Ga., and the Appalachian Mountain foothills in North Carolina. Most of these inland infestations have been traced to the shipment of infested wood products, particularly recycled railroad ties used in landscaping (Woodson et al. 2001; Forschler et al. 2001). It is difficult to predict how widespread their termite may ultimately become. Winter temperatures at locations north of Memphis, Tenn. (35* N Latitude) are thought to be too low for permanent establishment (Su and Scheffrahn 1990b). Nonetheless, the Formosan termite infestation in southern California appears to be expanding, despite environmental conditions presumed to be less than ideal for its survival (Haagsma et al. 1995).


Although its distribution is more limited than some Reticulitermes species, the Formosan subterranean termite poses a severe threat in areas where it is established. In Hawaii, this termite can cause major structural damage to a home in six months, and almost complete destruction within two years (Tamashiro 1984). In Louisiana, loss estimates approach $500 million, more than half occurring in the New Orleans area (Ring et al 2001). In the Southeast, field colonies commonly extend into the millions, foraging over distances in excess of 100 meters. Compared to the aforementioned species of Reticulitermes, Formosan termites generally cause more structural damage in a shorter period of time – primarily because populations tend to be larger – not because populations tend to be larger – not because individual termites eat wood faster (Su and Scheffrahn 2000). Population surveys in Florida indicated that C. Formosanus colonies tended to replace R. flavipes when foraging territories of both species overlapped (Su and Scheffrahn) 1988b).


Although C. formosanus ordinarily has colonies traceable to the soil, it may also form aerial nests with no ground connection. The nests are made of a material known as “carton,” consisting of soil and masticated wood cemented together with saliva and excrement. The nest carton helps to conserve water. Aboveground nests are often constructed in walls, attics, and roof areas of buildings. Walls often bulge outward as a result of the carton, which may be larger than a basketball.


Aerial infestations accound for about 25% of structural infestations by C. Formosanus in urban southeastern Florida (Su and Scheffrahn 1986b) and more than 50% of the high-rise buildings in Honolulu, Hawaii (Tamashiro et al. 1987). As with native subterranean species, true aerial infestations require remote moisture source, such as leaking roofs, faulty plumbing, or condensation from air conditioning units (Chambers et al. 1988). Moisture also appears to be precondition for nest founding by alates (Fei and Henderson 1999). Su et al. (1989) confirmed that Formosan termite swarmers are capable of re-infesting multi-story buildings by being carried upward on flat roofs by wind currents. Crevices in the roof surface allow moisture to accumulate and provide an entry point for the winged reproductive. Additional measure often must be taken to find and eradicate aerial nests, including removal and/or spot treatment of cartons or, in some cases, whole-structure fumigation.


Successful control of this formidable species requires proper identification. Formosan termite swarmers are larger than other subterranean species [1/2 inch (12-15mm) in length], and the body is yellowish brown. The wings are pale yellow and densely covered with tiny hairs. Swarming occurs on calm, humid evenings beginning around sundown. The swarmers are attracted to lights, with very large warms often seen around streetlights or the lights of buildings. In areas of Louisiana, “the exodus is so massive that baseball games have been canceled das the swarming insects gather by the lights” (Goyer and Henderson 1992).


The soldiers are also distinctive, the head being teardrop or oval shaped rather than elongated like most Reticulitermes species. Formosan termite soldiers also have a pore-like opening called a “fotanelle” on the fore margin of the head. When mud tubes or galleries are disturbed, the soldiers readily attack approaching objects (including one’s finger), and may emit a milky-white, sticky fluid from the fontanelle opening which can hinder the movement of enemies. Formosan termite colonies are also notable in having a higher proportion of solidiers (10%-15%), compared to most Reticulitermes species (1%-2%).


Besides infesting buildings, Formosan subterranean termites are serous pests of utility poles and railroad ties, often requiring removal and replacement. They have also been implicated in causing costly repairs and large-scale power outages in cities by eating through the insulation around underground telephone and electrical cables (Henderson and Dunaway 1999). More than 20 species of living trees and shrubs are attacked by C. formosanus in the vicinity of New Orleans alone, including oak, cypress, pine, and maple. Because the termites often hollow out and nest within trees and their base, structural support is diminished causing many to topple during high winds and storms. Using techniques initially developed in the 1960s, researches are working with pest management firms to inject infested trees and the adjoining soil with termiticides, often with excellent results (Henderson and Goyer 2000; Henderson 2001).


An interesting sidelight is the discovery that Formosan termite nest carton materials contains naphthalene, a common household fumigant for fabric insect pests (Chen et al. 1998). Naphthalene also has antimicrobial properties, and may be acting as both an inhibitor of harmful fungi and bacteria, and as a repellent against enemies such as ants. By “fumigating” their nests with naphthalene (apparently as a defense against enemies), the Formosan termite may also be contributing to its own demise. Researchers have developed a detection system to identify naphthalene within the walls of buildings, which ultimately could become a home detection system for termites (Henderson, 2002; personal communication).”


Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 248-251.
