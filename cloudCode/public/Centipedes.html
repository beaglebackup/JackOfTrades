Centipedes

Phylum Arthropoda, Class Chilopoda

“Centipedes are flattened, elongated animals with one pair of legs on most of their body segments. The total number of legs varies from 10 to more than 100, depending on the species. Centipedes have many-jointed antennae and jaws connected to poison glands to kill insects and other small creatures for food. The bite of a centipede, even the largest species found in the United States, is not sufficiently toxic to be lethal to humans, or even small children. It is usually comparable to a wasp sting (Smith 1982).

Ordinarily, centipedes dwell outdoors in damp locations, beneath accumulations of leaves, stones, boards, etc., and when disturbed they run swiftly. Individual centipedes sometimes crawl into the house and conceal themselves in various articles. Only the house centipede occurs in more than a few individuals within a building.

House Centipede. Back (1939a) noted the house centipede, Scutigera coleoptrata (Linnaeus), “has a wormlike body, an inch or more in length, with a pair of very long, slender antennae springing from the head, and with 15 pairs of long legs arranged along the sides of its body, the last pair being much more than twice the length of the body in the female.” The body of this grayish-yellow centipede is marked with three longitudinal dark stripers, and the legs are encircled with alternating dark and white bands. The last pair of hind legs is modified to “lasso” and hold its victims. There are six larval instars and four post-larval instars before maturity.

This centipede is now found throughout the United States, but it is native to Mexico. It lives outdoors and indoors, and in the home, the house centipede occurs in moist cellars, damp closets, and bathrooms, where it feeds on insects and spiders. During a one-year period, more than 200 house centipedes were found in a sump pump in the basement of a house near Pittsburgh. The weak jaws of the house centipede can penetrate the skin only with difficulty. Curran (1946) stated there are several records where the bite resulted in swelling and pain, but usually it is no worse than that of a bee sting.

“Giant” Centipedes. Some of the centipedes (Scolopendra spp.) of the Southeast and Southwest are impressive, sometimes reaching a length of 8 inches (20 cm). One of the most impressive is the giant desert, Scolopendra heros, found in Arizona and Southern California. This species has dark blue head and posterior “pseudohead” to confuse predators. If the “pseudohead” is grabbed, the real head is free to bite.

According to Smith (1982), the bite usually causes local inflammation, redness, and swelling – and occasionally systemic symptoms – that disappear in four to five hours. Occasionally, people bitten have reported “swelling and tenderness for several weeks with occasional suppuration and slow healing of the wound.” Logan et al. (1985) reported an incident of rhabdomyolysis (disintegration of skeletal muscle tissue) accompanied by acute renal failure following the bite of the giant desert centipede Scolopendra heros. Recovery was incomplete four weeks after the bite. Textbooks have described bites of scolopendrid centipedes as painful but not life threatening; however, they may be life threatening to certain presensitized individuals as seen with bee and wasp stings.”

Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 1076-1077
