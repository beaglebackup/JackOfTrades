Brownbanded Cockroach

Supella longipalpa Serville) (=S. suppellectilium[Serville])

“The name “brownbanded” cockroach comes from the pronounced transverse bands and coloration across the wings of the adults and bodies of the nymphs. This cockroach is similar to the German cockroach in appearance, but may be distinguished by the following characteristics:

There is greater sexual dimorphism, with females having distinctly larger abdomens and shorter wings then males.

The brownbanded cockroach lacks the two dark stripes on the prothorax found on the German Cockroach. Rather the pronotum has a black bell-shaped pattern with a translucent border.

The wings are twice-banded with brownish yellow stripes.

The egg capsule is smaller than that of the German cockroach, being but 3/16 inches (5 mm) long, and having about half as many eggs.

The brownbanded and German Cockroaches are similar in that they completely depend on the human habitat for survival, and they are the only known dedicated domestic cockroach species. The origin of the brownbanded cockroach is thought to be African. Rehn (1945). “This species hides in cupboards, pantries, or even in other rooms of the house. Preferences are shown for high locations such as shelves in closets, behind pictures and picture moldings, and the like. Egg cases may be deposited about the kitchen sink, desks, tables, and other furniture, and even in bedding. This habit of hiding egg cases in furniture probably accounts for its spread northward, for it has been found that infested premises follow a trip to the South, or transportation of goods from a southern state.” Gould & Deay (1940). Benson and Huber (1988) reported that brownbanded cockroaches have a preference for higher resting locations with 92.5% of all oothecae deposited in clusters of two or more. They also report that in a laboratory experiment, females selectively placed their egg cases in corrugated cardboard (74.5%), followed by sane (24.5%), and finally Plexiglas (1.0%).

In addition to being a pest in homes, the brownbanded cockroach is often found in office settings. This species does not have the same high moisture requirement that the German cockroach has, so it can survive in areas where access to free liquid may be minimal.

After insemination, the female develops an enormously enlarged abdomen as she becomes gravid. When fully developed the protruding egg capsule is yellowish or reddish-brown in color and is 3/16 inch (5 mm) in length. The female carries the mature egg capsule for 24 to 36 hours and then attaches it to some object. Females produce a greater number of egg cases during the summer. The brownbanded cockroach prefers temperatures of above 80* as temperatures below 75* retard its development (Gould and Deay 1940).

The sex pheromone of the brownbanded cockroach, called supellapyrone, has been identified and has markedly difference chemistry from the sex pheromone of other cockroach species (Charlton et al. 1993). Liang et al. (1998) evaluated the synthetic version of supellapyrone for use in detection and monitoring of brownbanded cockroaches and found that trap catch was enhanced six to 28 times compared with traps without pheromone. The authors concluded that supellapyrone offers a powerful monitoring and pest management tool for brownbanded cockroaches.

- Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 142-144.
