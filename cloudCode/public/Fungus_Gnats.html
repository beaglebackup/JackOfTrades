Fungus Gnats

Families Fungivoridae and Sciaridae

“Fungus gnats belong to the family Fungivoridae (formerly Mycetophilidae) and family Sciaridae. These flies are generally small, dark flies with long thin wings and legs. They are characterized by the inordinately long first segment of the legs called the coxa. The species of family Sciaridae are commonly referred to as the dark winged fungus gnats due to the smoky color of their wings.

According to Sollers (1950), fungus gnats, at times, occur in the home where ferns and other houseplants containing soil rich in humus serve as breeding source. Outdoors, the larvae live in fungi, damp soil, or decayed vegetable matter. In commercial buildings, fungus gnats can be found breeding in the soil in atriums as well as in potted plants. Usually the soil is overwatered which allows the growth of fungi on which the larvae feed. These flies are harmless, yet they are annoying when found indoors. They are attracted to lights are commonly seen in windows.

The control of fungus gnats in buildings is simple the soil or wetted site in which they are breeding must be found and then dried out. The top 2 or 3 inches (5.0 to 7.6 cm) of soil should be overturned to speed drying, after which the larvae will die. For potted plants, it is important to thoroughly clean the overflow sauces underneath pots as the larvae will breed in these plates. The watering schedule for the plants should be changed to prevent these conditions from recurring.

Hedges (1994) was consulted on a fungus gnat problem in a hotel. The hotel contained a large aviary atrium in which many valuable birds were housed. Bird feces in the soil, together with excess watering of plants, promoted the growth of fungi on which thousands of fungus gnat larvae were feeding. After a period of time, adult flies were found flying throughout the first two floors of the hotel. Insecticides could not be used because of the birds. The problem was solved by changing the plant watering schedule, installing large fans to blow over the surface of the soil to dry it out and raking the top several inches of soil to speed drying. Electric light traps were effective in attracting and killing large numbers of the remaining adult fungus gnats.

Baur (1984) related a story of a fungus gnat infestation not associated with soil. A geodesic dome home was constructed using foam insulation inside the walls. Evidently moisture was trapped inside with the foam insulation and allowed fungi to grow in the walls. The fungus gnats began breeding in large numbers in the walls and feeding on the fungi. The breeding site of the gnats was confirmed by placing tape around electric outlets that captured gnats as they attempted to exit. Covering the home with tarps and fumigating with methyl bromide eliminated the infestation. Baur noted that the problem might have been resolved without fumigating. Once the house was covered by tarps, the warm temperature of the day caused moisture in the walls to evaporate, drying out the gnat breeding area inside the walls.”

Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 856-857
