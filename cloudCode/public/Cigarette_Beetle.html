Cigarette Beetle

Lasioderma serricorne (Fabricius), Family Anobiidae


This cosmopolitan species infests tobacco wherever it is stored and is the most destructive pest found in stored tobacco. It is also a very serious pest books where it may cause injury to the binding and the leaves. Furniture upholstered with flax tow or straw also supported infestations of this beetle. The adults are strong sliers and will fly on late afternoons and dull cloudy days.


The foods upon which it feeds are legion and include rice, ginger, raisins, pepper, dried fish, dates, belladonna, drugs, seeds, cottonseed meal, processed grain products, rodent baits, and pyrethrum powder strong enough to kill cockroaches, Dried flowers may be a source of infestation in the house. This beetle is the chief offender in botanical circles where it is known as the “Herbarium beetle.” This beetle may also damage silk.


DESCRIPTION. The cigarette beetle is a small, squat beetle, measuring 1/8 inch (2 to 3 mm) in length, and is shiny reddish-brown. It resembles the drugstore beetle but is lighter colored and does not have the lines of pits on its wing covers. Its antennae are serrated while those of the drugstore beetle end in a three-segmented club.


LIFE HISTORY. The eggs are laid in the folds of newly harvested baled or bundled tobacco in storage, never in the tobacco leaves in the field. The eggs may also be laid at the open end of a cigar, etc., where they can be difficult to detect. The beetles become distributed widely throughout the factory in the hogsheads or in cigars to oviposit.


The eggs hatch six to 10 days after deposition. The larvae shun the light and make long cylindrical galleries through the leaves while feeding on the edges and center of the leaves. Although the larvae feed on a great variety of vegetable matter, they generally prefer cured tobacco leaves and manufactured tobacco. At about 60*F(16*C), the larvae become dormant and may then hibernate.


Five to six overlapping generations per year may occur in warm localities, but only one generation in more temperate regions. In the United States, three generations per year can occur. The adults are strong fliers and are active in subdued light at temperatures above 65*F(18*C). The winter is usually passed in the larval stage, but the adults, which are not very cold-tolerant, may secure themselves in some crevice.


The Fourth instar larvae are more tolerant of colder temperatures surviving below 45*F (7*C) for several weeks. More than six days is required to kill adult beetles at 40*F (4*C).
