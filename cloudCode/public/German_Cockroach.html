German Cockroach

(Blattella germanica)


BACKGROUND INFORMATION. There are over 3,500 species of cockroach worldwide. Of the 45 species in the Blattella genus, the German cockroach is the most common. They have adapted to be only able to survive around humans or human activity.


DISTRIBUTION & HABITAT. The German cockroach can be found worldwide and has become domesticated, adapted living primarily in structures and controlled temperatures. They can be spread through commerce, transportation and infested items, but also spread through migration, typically of gravid (egg bearing) females. German cockroaches are commonly found in homes, supermarkets, restaurants and other places with food sources. They are primarily in kitchens, bathrooms, and other humid places where they have daily access to water. They prefer to hide in wood and paper material and because they are also attracted to warmth will harbor in electrical appliances. Because of their flat shape, they are able to hide in cracks as small as 1/16th inch wide. The feces of German cockroaches, or frass, have an aggregation pheromone in them that attract other cockroaches to the area.


IDENTIFICATION. German cockroaches average 0.5-0.6 inches long and brown in color and have two dark stripes running from their head to wings. They have wings but are unable to fly. Nymphs resemble adults but are smaller and lack wings.


LIFE CYCLE. Male cockroaches are attracted to the female’s sex pheromones. After rubbing their antennae together, the female will consume nutrient rich secretions from the male tergal glands. The pair then mates. A female can mate more than once, but a she can fertilize all the eggs she produces in her lifetime with just a single mating.


The female produces an ootheca, an egg capsule less than a 1/4 inch long that contains 30-40 individual eggs. Females typically produced 4-8 egg capsules in her lifetime. The female cockroach carries the ootheca with her until the eggs are ready to hatch and then typically deposits them away from cockroaches to prevent cannibalism. The eggs have an average incubation period of 28 days, although that does vary greatly.


Once the eggs have hatched, the juvenile German cockroach goes through 6-7 instars before they become an adult. The time it takes to go through this phase is dependent on temperature, and can range from 54-215 days, with an average of 103 days for development. Once they become an adult, they typically live about 10 months. Populations of German cockroaches are usually 75% nymphs and 25% adults.


FEEDING BEHAVIOR. The German cockroach is an omnivore and a scavenger. They are commonly attracted to starch, sugary foods, grease and meat. They will become cannibalistic at times as well. German cockroaches are nocturnal, foraging at night and hiding in sheltered areas during the day.


HEALTH CONCERNS. Because of their presence around food and garbage, German cockroaches can pose health concerns from bacteria cross contamination. Pathogens that have been found on German cockroaches include Enterobacter aerogenes, Clostridium perfrigens (food poisoning), E. Coli, leprosy, Pseudomonas aeruginosa (respiratory infections; gastroenteritis), Salmonella, Serratia macescens (food poisoning), dysentery, staph infections, pneumonia, pinworms, whipworm, Amebiasis, and polio. These diseases can be carried externally on the cockroach’s body, bristles or sticky pads of the feet or internally within the gut and excrements.


Cockroaches can be a trigger for asthma. Children especially are at risk for cockroach related asthma and those with high cockroach levels are three times more likely to be hospitalized with asthma attacks. People can also exhibit other allergic reactions to cockroaches including skin rashes, watery eyes, congestion of nasal passages and runny nose.


Cockroaches can also be a stressor or a source of anxiety or shame. Because of their association with unsanitary conditions many people can feel shame for harboring cockroaches.
