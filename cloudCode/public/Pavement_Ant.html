Pavement Ant

Tetramorium caespitum (Linnaeus)

Subfamily Myrmicinae


IDENTIFYING CHARACTERISTICS. The workers are 2.5 to 3 mm long and monomophic (one size). They have two-segmented petiole, and a stinger. It is not known, however, if they can bite or sting (Thompson 1990). Their antennae are 12-segmented with a three-segmented club. The propodeum has a pair of small spines. The head and thorax are sculptured with numerous parallel grooves. The body color varies from light to dark brown to blackish (Smith 1965). Queens have similar appearance but are larger (6 mm)(Thompson 1990). Pavement ants move slowly.


DISTRIBUTION. Brown (1957) and Smith (1965) believed this ant to be of European or Asiatic origin, while Creighton (1950) considered it native to North America. Its range includes most of the U.S., and it is particularly common along the West Coast (Bennett et al 1997). In the Northeast and Midwest, it is the number one ant pest in commercial buildings (Hedges 1997).


BIOLOGY & HABITS. The pavement ant derives its name form its habit of nesting beside and under sidewalks, driveways, and foundations. New colonies are established after mating flights that usually take place in the spring but may occur at other times of the year if the ants are nesting indoors (Hedges 1997). The alates typically emerge from under baseboards, expansion joints, or from floor registers connected to heating ducts. In commercial buildings, they often become a nuisance when the alates emerge form openings in walls above false ceiling, then drop into the rooms below (Hedges 1992). This activity may go on for weeks as more alates emerge.


Nests are located in soil in the open or under stones and pavement, and in masonry or rotting wood (Smith 1965). The ants usually leave conspicuous piles of excavated soil. During winter, they will move inside, preferably to be near to a heat source such as radiator or heating duct (Hedges 1997).


Pavement Ants tend homopterans for honeydew, especially subterranean forms, and feed on live and dead insects and a variety of plants (Smith 1965). As household pests, they are attracted to both greasy and sweet foods (Bennett et al. 1997).
