Indian Meal Moth

Plodia Interpunctella (Hbn.), FamilyPyralidae


“Indian meal moths are general feeders upon grain and grain products, dried fruits, seeds, graham crackers, nuts, powered milk, chocolate, candies, etc. In homes, dried red peppers, bird seed, and dehydrated dog food containing meat and cereal are commonly infested. This pest is especially important and very destructive wherever dried fruits are stored. It is a native of the Old World and presently cosmopolitan in distribution. A few larvae in a package with their webbing and frass are very repulsive to homeowners, and costly to the firm marketing the material. Figs in particular are readily infested.


Although it is selfdom as pest of milling machinery, like the Mediterranean flour moth, its webbing will in time become sufficiently abundant in previously injured grain to become noticeable. Since it prefers the coarser grades of flour, it is the most common insect in packages of whole wheat, graham flour, and corn meal. The larva webs together the flour with strands of silk, forming clumps.


The Indian meal moth is the most common food-infesting moth found in homes, grocery stores, and any place where dried pet foods are produced or stored. Cox and Bell (1991) noted that this moth “has the widest distribution of all moths generally infested stored foods and is truly a global pest.” Home infestations can be traced back to nearly any food source but commonly originate in dried pet food or bird seed. Nuts are also a common breeding source, especially the nut caches of squirrels in attics and chimneys. Dried flour arrangements containing seed-bearing stalks are also susceptible. Indian meal moths can also enter homes from outdoor sources such as seeds or nuts found on the ground, rodent and squirrel nests or from seed in bird feeders.


DESCRIPTION. Indian meal moths are the easiest food-infesting moth to identify. The wing span is about 5/8 inch (16 mm) and the outer half to one-third of the front wing is covered with reddish-copper scales. These scales, however, are often rubbed off and are indistinct in older specimens. Under magnification, any remaining reddish-copper colored scales can be seen on the wings.


LIFE HISTORY. This species commences the year with a brood of overwintering larvae that pupate mostly in March and emerge in April. The generations overlap as the seasons progress. The female begins oviposition at night about three days after emerging. The eggs are grayish-white and 0.3 to 0.5 mm in length.


Upon hatching, the larvae disperse. Within a few hours, they establish themselves on the crevices of fruit or enter packaging through cracks. The larva is 3/8 to ¾ inch (9 to 19 mm) in length, with an average of about 5/8 inch (13 mm). Its color is usually dirty white, but may range from pink to brown to a greenish tinge. The larvae feed in or near a tunnel-like case of frass and silk they web together. They then emerge from their food source and can travel some distance before spinning their cocoons in crevices or at wall/ceiling junctions. Pupation usually occurs in the vicinity of their food. The sex ratio is, on average, 1:1, but the emergence of males and females of a particular generation can be displaced by food shortage, the females tending to emerge later.”
