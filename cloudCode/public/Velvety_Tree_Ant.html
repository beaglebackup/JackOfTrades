Velvety Tree Ant

Liometopum occidentale

Subfamily Dolichodorinae

Identifying Characteristics. “Workers range in size from 2.5 to 5.5 mm long. They have a one-segmented petiole, and the abdomen is covered with very fine hairs, which gives them a velvety appearance. Although they may superficially resemble carpenter ants, they belong to a difference subfamily.

Distribution. Velvety tree ants are found in the western and southwestern states from Colorado through Arizona, California and the Pacific Northwest. Liometopum occidentale is distributed along the West Coast from Oregon to Baja California, from sea level to 4800 feet (Snelling and George 1979). The closely related L. luctuosum ranges from Wyoming to western Texas, Nevada, and California, and into Mexico (Gulmahamad 1995). Regional common names for this species include black velvety, pine tree an, and silky carpenter ant.

Biology & Habits. In their natural habitat, nests of velvety tree ants are typically under stones or in hollow trees. Infestations resemble those of carpenter ants, but the excavated materials has a finer texture. To build the nest chambers, which are composed of paper-like material called carton, the ants mix pieces of plant material and soil and add a secretion that cements it together. Brood chambers are maintained within this matrix. The ants often congregate in hollows within the insulation or wood, forming temporary “resting places” or bivouacs for the foragers. Damage to insulation can be severe, but damage to wood has not been documented. Attempts to located nests indoors are often unsuccessful because the “nests” are the temporary bivouacs, which are vacated or moved as foraging territories change.

Velvety tree ants are aggressive and will attack and spray intruders with secretions that resemble those of odorous house ants. Homeowners often complain about strong odors associated with an infestation of velvety tree ants.

Trails of large colonies can be 2 to 3 cm wide but are often hidden from view. Velvety tree ants tend homopterans for honeydew, prey on other arthropods, and also scavenge on dead animals.”

Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 666-667
