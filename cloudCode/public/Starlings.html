Starlings

(European)

Class Aves, Order Passeriformes, Family Sturnidae

“Starlings are disliked in urbanized settings because of their raucous vocalizations made at roosting time and because of filth (e.g., feces and nesting materials) they leave behind. Starlings also create problems by nesting on or inside buildings. Starlings have adapted well to cities and suburbs where they utilize a multitude of roosting sites, including building ledges, lighted signs, marquees, billboard bracing, hollow lamp posts, soffits, as well as dryer and stove exhaust ducts and vents. Thousands of starlings may invade a city at dusk and roost side by side, forming solids rows of birds. Staring also roost in trees, taking on a pest status when the trees are in a city park or close to human habitation. Not only is the roost obnoxious to the senses, but the birds are known to transmit diseases such as encephalitis, ornithosis, and histoplasmosis. Starlings, in addition to consuming large amounts of poultry, hog, and cattle feed, are implicated in the spread of diseases of live-stock (e.g. hog cholera) via fecal contamination.

Lawns infested with the larvae of turfgrass pest insects are likely to be visited by flocks of starlings which feed on insects and, in the process, punch thousands of unsightly holes in the sod. More costly damage is inflicted by hungry starlings on cereal, fruit, and vegetable crops in agricultural, orchard and garden settings.”

Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Page 1004
