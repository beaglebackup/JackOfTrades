Tropical Rat Mite

(Ornithonyssus bacoti)

Background Information. There are over 45,000 species of mites. Mites are closely related to spiders and ticks.

Distribution & Habitat. Because of their association with Norway rats and other rodents, tropical rat mites can be found worldwide. Although, as the name suggests, they do prefer warmer climates. Tropical rat mites live in and around the rodent nests and prefer to be near heat sources like hot water pipes or stoves. If the host animal dies or the population of mites becomes too large for the nest they will move to these warm areas and travel to find other blood meals.

Identification. Tropical rat mites are very small, 1-1.5mm in length, usually compared to a period at the end of sentence. Because of their small size, they are typically identified by the presence of a host animal. Tropical rat mites are associated with rodents like Norway rats, roof rats and the house mouse.

Life Cycle. Within three days of becoming an adult, a the mite will feed and mate. The female lays eggs about two days after mating and hatch in about a day and a half. Females can lay up to 100 eggs in her lifetime. The larvae need a blood meal from the host before they can molt into the second nymphal stage. They can then molt from the second nymphal stage to adult when it is between 68° to 72°F. The entire development takes about 11-12 days and requires at least 4 blood meals.

Feeding & Behavior. Tropical rat mites have been found to survive up to 63 days without a blood meal.

Health Concerns. On humans, bites can be found anywhere, but typically occur in warm and constricted area like around clothing waistbands, armpits and under the breasts. Tropical rat mites can cause painful, itchy bites and dermatitis. They are not known to transfer any diseased to humans, but secondary skin infections can occur from scratching.
