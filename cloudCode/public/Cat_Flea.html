Cat Flea

(Ctenocephalides felis)

Background Information. There are at least 2,500 species of fleas worldwide. Fleas are the main source of transmission of the plague, although it is most commonly found in the Ground Squirrel Flea and the Oriental Rat Flea. The plague, known as the black death, killed between one quarter to one third of Europe during the middle ages, and is still known to cause approximately a dozen deaths in the United States each year. 90% of the fleas found on cats and dogs in North America are cat fleas.

Distribution & Habitat. Adult fleas use tarsal hooks to attach to their host animal, commonly a cat or dog. Fleas are found on or near the bedding area of the host animal.

Identification. Adult cat fleas are 1-2 mm in length and typically reddish brown to black in color.

Life Cycle. 36-48 hour after females have a blood meal, they will then lay approximately 10 eggs, each 1/50 inch long and white in color. The eggs are not sticky and typically fall through the host’s coat. They will hatch out into larva in typically 2-10 days. Larva go through three instars, during which they will eat any available organic material. Larvae will eat the fecal matter of adults, also known as “flea dirt”. The larval stage goes from 3 weeks to 3 months, depending on environmental conditions. Fleas then form a cocoon to become a pupa, a phase that can last as little as a week or several months if they need to overwinter. The silk the cocoon is made of is sticky and attracts debris or dirt so it camouflages in the environment. The cocoons must be stimulated through the presence of a host via movement, heat or carbon dioxide before they can emerge as adults. From egg to adult can happen in as little as 12 days or up to several months. Adult fleas can live up to a year when feeding on regular meals.

Feeding Behavior. Fleas feed on the blood of warm blooded animals. Their primary hosts are cats, although they will feed on dogs, humans and mammals and birds. Cat fleas typically need their host animal, usually dog or cat, to complete their lifecycle, but females can produce eggs off human blood if given consistent access to a food source. A female flea can consume up to 15 times her body weight each day.

Health Concerns. Fleas are known to transmit several different diseases. Cat fleas have been found to carry typhus and tapeworms. The bites of fleas can also be extremely itchy and painful to the host animal.
