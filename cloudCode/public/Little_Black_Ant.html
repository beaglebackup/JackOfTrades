Little Black Ant

Monomorium minimum (Buckley)

Subfamily Myrmicinae


IDENTIFYING CHARACTERISTICS. Workers are monomorphic and small, 1.5 to 2 mm in length. Their antennae are 12 segmented with a three segmented club. They are dark brown to black and have a shiny appearance.


DISTRIBUTION. This ant is found in the northeastern U.S. to the Pacific Coast and south-central U.S. from west Tennessee to central Texas (Hedges 1997).


BIOLOGY & HABITS. Colonies may contain several queens and be heavily populated (Wheeler and Wheeler 1986). Alates have been recorded from June to August (Smith 1965). The little black ant is common in wooded habitats. Most infestations originate from outdoors and can be traced to a stump, tree, log, fence, or a pile of lumber or bricks. They also nest in landscape mulch and under stones. Hedges (1997) inspected one home where trails of little black ants ringed the house, entering it from multiple locations. A careful inspection revealed that the trail originated from a nearby tree. Indoors colonies may nest in the voids of walls and cabinets, as well as within and behind foundations and brick or stone veneer.


The little black ant feeds on insects, honeydew, plant exudates, pollen, and may even kill hatchling birds (Smith 1965). It also feeds on bird feces (Hedges 1997). Indoors, little black ants feed on a variety of foods including sweets, meats, grease, bread, oils, cornmeal, fruits, and fruit juices (Smith 1965).
