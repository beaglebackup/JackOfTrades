Ticks

“Ticks are blood-sucking ectoparasites that cause significant skin lesions such as papules, nodules, and extensive secondarily infected areas of tissue. In addition to bite reactions, they are capable of transmitting a wide variety of disease organisms, such as protozoa (babesiosis), viruses (encephalitis and Colorado tick fever), and bacteria (Rocky Mountain spotted fever, ehrlichiosis, Lyme Disease, and tularemia) (Spach 1993; Goddard 2000). They seem to be especially associated with spotted fever group rickettsial organisms (Burgdorfer 1977; Goddard 2001). In addition, ticks may cause paralysis in humans and animals as a result of salivary toxins injected upon feeding.

Ticks and mites belong to the class Arachnida, which also includes spiders, scorpios, psuedoscopions, windscorpions, and other similar groups. Some species of arachnids are important parasites and vectors of human and animal diseases. Others are known for their bites or stings, which can be painful or even fatal if not properly attended.

Of the eight generally recognized groupings of terrestrial arachnids, the Acarina (Acari), or mites and ticks, are considerably important to people because they parasitize humans and domestic animals, sometimes drawing sufficient blood in the process to weaken or kill (for instance, the fowl ticks). These arachnids may transmit disease pathogens such as those causing Rocky Mountain spotted fever, Lyme disease, and tularemia (hard ticks), and relapsing fever (soft ticks). They may cause allergic reactions such as dust mite allergy.

Ticks and mites are not segmented, and the body is not divided by constrictions (as in insects and spiders). The larval stage has three pair of legs, but the (one or more) nymphal stages and adults have four pairs. Mouthparts consist of a pair of chelicerae and two short palps. The body is divided into two regions: the first being the gnathosoma (containing the mouthparts) and the rest of the body making up the idiosoma. Males are usually distinct in appearance from females.“

“Ticks have a leathery body.

They are usually 3mm or more in length.

Ticks have a sensory organ on the top of the tarsi of legs.

Ticks are ectoparasites on mammals, birds, and some on reptiles and amphibians.

Hypostome barbed – It is this structure which makes the removal of the tick difficult.

Ticks have 1-8 nymphal stages.”
