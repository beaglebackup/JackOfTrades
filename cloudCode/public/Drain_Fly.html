Drain Fly

(Psychoda pacifica)


BACKGROUND INFORMATION. Also known as moth flies because of their appearance, drain flies get their name because of their habitat.


DISTRIBUTION & HABITAT. Psychoda pacifica are the species of drain fly that occur on the west coast of the United States, from Southern California to Alaska. As their name implies, drain flies are typically found near drains and sewers. In residential facilities they are typically found in sinks and bathtubs. In commercial facilities, they are found in and around sinks, floor drains and other areas where moisture and organic matter are allowed to accumulate. Drain flies are able to survive in any place where moistures is allowed to sit for a week or more.


IDENTIFICATION. Drain flies are 1/12 inch long and are light tan in color and with brownish-grey wings. The wings are densely covered with long hairs that lend to the “moth fly” name. They have long antennae with 13 segments.


LIFE CYCLE. Adult flies mate shortly after emerging from their pupal stage. Females lay masses of 30-100 eggs which hatch out in 32-48 hours, depending on temperature. The larval stage lasts 9 to 15 days, during which the larva will feed on sediment, decaying vegetation and microscopic flora and fauna. Larva and pupa are aquatic or semi aquatic. After undergoing a brief pupal phase they emerge as adult flies. The length of the life cycle is dependent on temperature, but typically lasts anywhere from 8 to 24 days. Adult drain flies live about two weeks.


FEEDING BEHAVIOR. Drain flies feed on decaying matter and microscopic flora and fauna.


HEALTH CONERNS. Despite their association with sewers and other unsanitary locations, drain flies are not known to spread any specific diseases.
