Western Subterranean Termite

Reticulitermes Hesperus Banks


This termite is as abundant in the western United States as R. flavipes is in the East. It is commonly encountered along the entire Pacific Coast from British Columbia south into lower California and western Mexico and eastward into Idaho and Nevada.


The biology and habits of R. Hesperus are similar to those of the eastern subterranean termite. As with other Reticulitermes species, colony growth from a primary pair is low. Even under highly favorable conditions swarms cannot be expected before the third or fourth year. Pickens (1932) and Weesner (1956) reviewed the biology, colony initiation, and economic significance of this species.


The eastern and western subterranean termites are similar in overall appearance, although R. Hesperus swarmers tend to be a bit larger. It is important to be able to distinguish this species from the western drywood termite, Incisitermes minor, since their swarming periods overlap. (I. minor swarmers are somewhat larger, and the head and thorax are reddish brown.) In California, swarming usually occurs on warm, sunny days during autumn, winter, or early spring since this corresponds with the rainy season (Ebeling 1978). As with eastern subterranean termites, swarming takes place during the daytime.


Foraging activity of R. Hesperus in California increases in late spring, peaks in summer and is minimal during winter. Population studies with Reticulitermes in California further suggest there may be numerous relatively small colonies coexisting in the same area with populations ranging from 5,000-500,000 foragers (Haagsma and Rust 1995; Haverty et al. 1999a, 2000). Field studies in southern California found that foraging populations of R. Hesperus were significantly larger in urban populated areas than in undeveloped native scrub sage habitats. Plentiful irrigation and an abundance of trees, shrubs, and wood framed structures in populated areas apparently afforded ideal conditions for population growth. Smith and Rust (1994) observed that R. Hesperus workers in southern California preferred soil temperatures between 84*F to 90*F (29*C to 32*C) and were never found at temperatures above 104*F (40*C). They concluded that termites foraging close to the soil surface might seek refuge from the heat under objects large enough to cast thermal shadow. Building could be providing an ideal refuge from hot temperatures, and thus, concentrating foraging populations in the vicinity of structures.


Although the western subterranean termite has been considered the predominant termite species in California, recent findings suggest there may actually be multiple. Closely related Reticulitermes species previously all thought to be R. Hesperus (Haverty and Nelson 1997; Haverty 1999b). Sophisticated new diagnostic techniques, many involving DNA “fingerprinting,” will almost certainly reveal additional new termite species in the future.”
