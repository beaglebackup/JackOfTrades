Oriental Cockroach

Blatta orientalis (Linneaus)


“The oriental cockroach is more temperate in origin than most pest species of cockroach, Cornwell (1968) identified it as originally a native of Northern Africa. It is not successful in tropical climates and in the United States is found only rarely in Florida (Atkinson et at. 1990). The oriental cockroach prefers temperatures below 84* F (Cornwell 1968), and it exhibits sufficient cold tolerance to survive outdoors through temperate winters with access to food and water (le Patourel 1993).


The oriental cockroach is so dark brown in color that it is often referred to as the “black beetle” in both the United States and England. Very often this cockroach as well as other species, is called a “water bug,” possibly because these cockroaches inhabit damp places. The adult is one inch (25 mm) in length, and the male differs in appearance from the female. The male has fully developed brownish-black wings covering about 75% of the abdomen, but males do not fly. The shiny black female has rudimentary wings that are reduced to “wing stubs.” The mature female is distinguished from the large nymph by the presence of venation on these wing stubs. Laing (1938) stated the abdomen of the female is broader than the male and appear to be dragged along the floor when the insect is in motion, while the male keeps its body clear of a surface when running. The male is easily distinguished from the female by the presence of fully developed wings and by the styli located between its pairs of jointed cerci. Females are more numerous than males, and mating occurs anytime during the year.


This species often travels through sewer pipes and lives on filth (anonymous 1957). It may enter the home in food packages and laundry, or come in under the door, through air ducts, ventilators, or garbage chutes. The species is attracted to wet and shady perimeter areas and prefers the dark and dampness of basements and crawlspaces.

The oriental cockroach prefers temperatures in the range of 68*F to 84* (Cornwell 1968). This range is typically cooler than the preferred temperatures for other cockroach species. Consequently, the oriental cockroach may be the sole cockroach inhabitant of cooler basements and cellars. Oddly, this cockroach can also tolerate hot, drier locations if regular access to water is available (Cornwell 1968).


The female may deposit from one to 18 egg capsules with an average of eight per lifetime. The egg capsule is carried from 12 hours to five days and then deposited where food is readily available. Rau (1945) showed this cockroach prefers to fee upon starchy foods.”


Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 144-146
