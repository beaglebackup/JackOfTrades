Itch of Scabies Mite

Family Sarcoptidae

Sarcoptes scabei var. hominis (DeGeer)

“This is but one of a number of varieties of the same species (Arlian 1989). There can be “horse scabies” or “dog scabies” which are actually the same species but not adapted to humans. Sarcoptes scabei is of greater interest to the doctor than to the pest management professional since its treatment and control must be accomplished under a physician’s care. It is considered briefly at this point, however, since symptoms due to the itch mite may be confused with those caused by other mites and insects.

The itch mite is from 0.25 to 0.5 mm in length and is commonly found in the webbing between fingers and the folds of the wrists, on the elbows, behind the knees, around the nipples, and on and around the genitals. The female burrows beneath the outer layer of skin, leaving at first a small papule, but if allowed to continue, will leave red sinuous trails along the skin for perhaps as much as 2 to 3 cm. Perhaps one of the interesting facets of this pest is that, even after successful medical treatment, affected people may continue to experience severe itching for up to a month. On the other hand, newly infested humans rarely experience any itching or discomfort (this is related to the developing immune response). For this reason, scabies infections may be well established by the time infestation is apparent and treatment is sought.

The entire life cycle from egg to adult takes from nine to 11 days for the males and from 14 to 17 days for the females.

Mellanby (1943) found females mites removed from the skin walked about fairly rapidly at 68°F (20°C). He found they could easily climb polished surfaces such as glass. At 90% relative humidity and 55°F (13°C), about 50% of the mites survived a week off-host and 5% lived for two weeks. Warm dry air causes rapid death. At 82°F (28°C) most mites died within 24 hours and none survived longer than two days. Transmission of mites is almost entirely by intimate contact.”

Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 473-475.
