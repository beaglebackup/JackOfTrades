Drugstore Beetle

Stegobium paniceum (Linnaeus), Family Anobiidae


The drugstore beetle is a common pest of the home and storehouse and is worldwide in distribution. It was given the Latin name paniceum because it feeds on bread, but its appetite includes practically everything edible and many things nonedible to people. In the household, it feeds on flour, meal, breakfast foods, spices (e.g., red pepper and sundried tomatoes), pet foods, horn and antler, and green coffee beans. This insect has even infested toxic materials such as wheat poisoned with strychnine.


It is also a serious pest of books and manuscripts and has been known to bore in a straight line through a whole shelf of books. It has been recorded feeding on a mummy and has been known to perforate tin foil, lead sheeting, and wood.


DESCRIPTION. This beetle is cylindrical in form, measures about 1/10 inch (2.5 mm) long, and is uniform brown in color. The head is not visible from above, and its legs and antennae remain pressed to the body when the insect is at rest, thus enabling it to escape detection. Adult drugstore beetles resemble cigarette beetles but are easily recognized by the presence of rows of deep pits on their wing covers. The wing covers on a cigarette beetle have not pits. LIFE HISTORY. The female deposits eggs singly in a suitable food media. In powdery substances, the larva forms a little round ball or cell that becomes its pupal cocoon. Four broods per year may result in a fairly warm atmosphere. Under cool conditions, only one generation per year is produced. Adults are not known to feed on food product, but can chew through packaging materials.
