Yellow Jacket

(Vespula vulgaris)


BACKGROUND INFORMATION. Yellow jackets are a type of wasp, a family that also includes hornets.


DISTRIBUTION & HABITAT. Yellow jackets build nest underground but will also build papery nests in wall voids or hanging from trees or structures. Papery nests are built from chewing wood fibers into a papery pulp. Nests begin small, but can eventually house 3,000 to 15,000 individuals at a time.


IDENTIFICATION. Yellow jacket workers are 3/4 inch long, and queens are a bit larger, about 1 inch long. As their name suggests, they are yellow with black markings. They have a narrow “waist” between the thorax and abdomen.


LIFE CYCLE. Egg bearing females, queens, lay single eggs into individual cells of the nest, usually 10-20 at a time. In a year, a nest can contain up to 25,000-30,000 eggs. Once the eggs hatch out, the eggs hatch, the queen will feed the larvae pre-chewed food. The larvae then use the cells to create cocoons to become pupae. The larvae and pupa phase takes approximately a month. Once they have become adults, the yellow jackets become workers and take over all duties from the queen except egg laying. Most of the nest will die off in colder months, b queens will overwinter in stumps, bark, leaf litter or shelters and form a new nest the following spring.


FEEDING BEHAVIOR. Depending on the species, yellow jackets are scavengers or predators. Adults typically feed on fruit and other sweet items like nectar, sap and honeydew, but larvae are carnivores that will feed on other insects, spiders or carrion. The workers feed the larvae and in return the larvae secrete a sugary substance for the adults.


HEALTH CONCERNS. While yellow jacket stings are painful, people only have strong reactions if they are allergic to the venom or has been stung several times.
