Termites In General

“Throughout history, humans have obligingly built wooden structure and made them accessible to termites. The Romans have referred to these insects as “Termes” which aptly means “woodwork.” Termites are among the the most ancient insects. Their fossilized remains have been found in formations more than 100 million years old, and they likely evolved from primitive wood-eating cockroaches. To this day, their diet has consisted solely of wood and other cellulose-containing materials. So the impression of the unfortunate homeowner – that termites have suddenly appeared like some ravenous horde from another planet – is rather shortsighted.

Termites are social insects, living in cooperative and cohesive colonies. In termite societies, the efforts of each member are directed toward the success of the whole. Individuals are relatively long-lived (worker termites, for example, may live about two years), colonies are self-perpetuating, and have a constantly available supply of food. Moreover, termites are protected from enemies and extremes of heat, cold, storms, etc. by their cryptic (hidden) way of life. The resiliency and destructive potential of termites is also related to their abundance. While small termite colonies may number in the thousands, large can contain millions of individuals (Grace et al. 1989; Su et al. 1993). Grace (1992) likened the foraging impact of this many termites to that of a large grazing animal, able to ingest almost 1.1 lb (0.5 kg) of wood each day.

Termites do more than merely provide work for pest management professionals. In nature, termites are very beneficial since they recycle wood and other cellulose-containing materials that other organisms cannot. Feeding be termites enhances the decomposition of organic matter and the return of nutrients to the soil. Their tunneling activities help to aerate soils and promote plant growth. As a by product of their digestion, termites also produce enormous amounts of methane. Since methane is a “greenhouse” gas that accumulates in the upper atmosphere, termite activity might even have wide-ranging effects on global warming and climate.

Birds, reptiles, amphibians, mammals, ants, and other arthropods consume termites as an important part of their diet. Among many cultures of the world, humans also partake of termites, which provide an efficient and ready source of protein and calories. Throughout Africa, termites are popular food item roasted, dried, ground into powder, and added to food as a paste (Berenbaum 1995; Pearce 1997). According to Pearce, some African tribes even use termites as oracles. Important decisions can be made by placing a piece of wood into a termite mound and noting whether or not it is eaten.”

What is a Termite? When termite-infested wood is broken into, one’s first impression is of small, nondescript white maggots with legs. A closer look will reveal creatures that are far more interesting. Like other insects, termites have six legs and a body divided in three main regions: head, thorax, and abdomen. Situated on the head is a pair of straight antennae made up of tiny bead-like segments. Also on the head is a pair of mandibles used for eating, defense and other functions. Termites are sensitive to stimulation by touch, odor, taste, and pressure. They detect such stimuli with tactile hairs and other specialized sense organs on various parts of their bodies.

Individuals within a termite colony may appear quite different and perform specialized tasks. These life forms, known as castes, include workers, soldiers and reproductives. The workers are usually the most numerous individuals in the colony, and the caste feeds upon wood. Worker termites are pale, soft-bodied, and prone to desiccation. In order to conserve body moisture, they must dwell underground or within wood, away from desiccating air currents and rays of the sun.

A key difference between termites and other social insects, such as bees and ants, is that termites have no pupal stage. Furthermore, immature termites look like tiny versions of the adults as is true of cockroaches and crickets. Another unique feature of termites versus other social insects is that the male (king) termite does not die after mating and remains with the female (queen as a long-time colony member. Termites are classified in the insect Order ‘Isoptera’ (iso meaning equal; ptera meaning wings) – so-names because the primary reproductive, commonly referred to as swarmers, have two pairs of almost identical wings.”
