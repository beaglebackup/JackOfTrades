Flesh Flies

Family Sarcophagidae


“Flesh flies, or sarcophagid flies, are much larger than house flies, usually about 1/3 inch (8 mm) long or slightly longer. They are grayish in color, with three distinct dark stripes on the thorax and a gray and black checkerboard pattern on the abdomen. Some species are a nuisance around dog kennels where the larvae feed on meat scraps or dog feces.


Hedges (1944) pointed out that, as their name suggests, flesh flies will breed in animal carcasses. Like blow flies and bottle flies, these flies are usually the first insects to arrive at a carcass after an animal dies, and in this role they are important insects in nature. Forensic entomologists use the larvae of flesh flies and blow flies collected at the site where a murder victim is found to help pinpoint the time of death.


Once the eggs have been laid on a suitable breeding material, the larvae hatch out and burrow under the surface. It is not uncommon to encounter the body of a dead mouse, for example, whose body writhes and wiggles as the maggots move about inside while feeding. After maturing in a few days, the larvae crawl out of the breeding material to pupate. They generally crawl only a short distance before burrowing into the soil to pupate. In one very interesting case, Ehmann (1977) observed a nighttime fish kill in a large backyard pond in the San Francisco Bay area. Before the end of the following day the dead goldfish floating on their sides in the pond were covered with sarcophagid eggs.


Sarcophaga albrichi Park. Occurs throughout the southern Canada and much of the eastern and Midwestern United states and is a pest of the southern United States. Mallis (1973) found S. aldrichi to be a very annoying pest in resort areas in Somerset Country, Pa., in late June. Some individuals referred to these flies as “red_eyed” flies because of their prominent red eyes. Although they did not bite, they sat on the skin and hovered around in annoying numbers. In these cases, the flies usually disappear within several weeks without any treatment. If necessary, spraying or fogging nearby trees with an appropriately labeled insecticide may be helpful.


The larvae of S. aldrichi are important predators of the pupae of the forest tent caterpillar, Malacosoma disstria Hubner, a common pest of sugar maples. When there is a heavy infestation of forest tent caterpillars, the adult sarcophagid flies may appear in great numbers. This is a case of a beneficial insect becoming a pest due to sheer numbers.”


Handbook of Pest Control; Mallis, Ninth Edition. Pages 838-839
